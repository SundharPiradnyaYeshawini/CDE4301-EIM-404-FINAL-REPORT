1. Introduction

1.1 Context and Motivation

Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) is widely recognised as an intermediate stage between normal age-related cognitive decline and dementia. (Eshkoor et al.,2015). It is characterised by noticeable deficits in memory, attention, and judgment that are greater than expected for a person’s age, yet not severe enough to significantly interfere with daily activities (Cipriani et al.2020). Individuals with MCI can carry out daily activities, but may experience increasing difficulty in performing complex tasks.

MCI is a critical focus area for research because it represents a potential window for early intervention before irreversible neurodegeneration occurs. While some individuals may recover normal cognitive function, numerous longitudinal studies have shown that people with MCI are at a significantly higher risk of progressing to dementia or Alzheimer’s disease (Petersen et al., 2018; Jongsiriyanyong & Limpawattana, 2018).

In Singapore, approximately 12.5% of adults above the age of 60 are estimated to have MCI. (Subramaniam et al., 2015). As the population ages, healthcare burdens are expected to increase significantly. The Ministry of Health projects that up to 152,000 people could be living with dementia by 2030, incurring an additional S$106 million in annual healthcare expenditure. Individuals with cognitive impairment incur an additional $700 of healthcare costs annually, largely due to increased emergency care visits and hospitalisation. (Duke-NUS, 2024) Another study has shown that the median annual cost of care for cognitively impaired patients is approximately S$13,800, driven largely by repeated medical consultations, imaging, and long-term management. (Woo et al., 2017)

The growing prevalence of MCI and dementia highlights the urgent need for early, accessible, and cost-effective detection methods. Table 1 summarises current technology available in relation to the detection of MCI.

Current screening and diagnostic procedures include Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) or Positron Emission Tomography (PET) brain scans, neuropsychological testing, and biochemical analysis of cerebrospinal fluid. While these are definitive and accurate, they are expensive, invasive, and resource-intensive. These hinder widespread adoption of screening for MCI, especially for early or community-based screening. Additionally, such assessments are typically performed only after cognitive symptoms become clinically apparent. Early diagnosis remains a challenge, as subtle physiological and behavioural changes frequently go unnoticed in routine clinical visits. (Wenier et al.,2023)

Given these challenges, there is a need to develop a non-invasive, scalable, and affordable tool that can continuously monitor subtle indicators of cognitive decline.

1.3  Project Objectives  

This project is conducted in collaboration with Professor Wu Changsheng’s laboratory, which partners with clinicians and students from the Saw Swee Hock School Of Public Health (SSHSPH). It forms part of a larger ongoing research program aimed at developing a non-invasive wearable mechano-acoustic sensor system to detect gait and speech-based biomarkers associated with MCI.

The overarching problem statement for the ongoing research is: “How might we make the detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment more timely, affordable, and non-invasive?”.

MCI is often underdiagnosed due to reliance on clinical assessments that are time-consuming, resource-intensive, and typically conducted only after noticeable symptoms arise. This creates a gap between early physiological changes and clinical detection.

The collaborative research project aims to address that gap by exploring whether gait and speech-based biomarkers, captured using wearable sensors, can serve as early indicators of cognitive decline.

Importantly, the system created by the research team is intended as a screening tool rather than a diagnostic device for MCI. The long-term goal of the collaborative research project is to enable community-wide non-invasive screening for early cognitive decline, allowing individuals with abnormal patterns to be referred for further clinical evaluation.

1.4 Project Scope

Within the broader research programme, multiple teams focus on different components, including the sensor hardware, data collection, data processing, clinical validation, and deployment strategies.

Overall Research Programme Context:

Professor Wu’s laboratory has developed a mechano-acoustic wearable sensor (“Research Sensor”) to capture vibration and motion signals from the throat and lower limb. This sensor consists of a battery-operated three-axis accelerometer encapsulated with silicone, and is used in collaboration with SSHSPH to collect participant data for research purposes.

Presently, all data collection is conducted in a controlled clinical setting, where speech assessments and gait measurements are conducted in-clinic under supervision. The collected data is reviewed and analysed by the research team to further refine algorithms and extract clinically relevant features.

Group-Specific Scope:

Within this larger ecosystem, the scope of this Final Year Project (as part of the Innovation and Design Programme in the National University of Singapore) is strictly focused on data analysis and feature extraction.

Specifically, this project aims to:

  • Extract gait parameters from accelerometer signals
  • Analyse speech-related vibration signals to derive meaningful features
  • Validate whether these features can be reliability obtained using only accelerometer data
  • Compare extracted features against established reference systems (e.g. motion-capture, video-based, and audio-based analysis).

Due to the developmental stage of the research, this study is conducted on healthy individuals only. The goal is to establish a robust analytical foundation of accelerometer data before progressing to patient population. This is essential because the research sensor developed captures only accelerometer data, and the team must first ensure that meaningful features can be derived from these signals.

Although the current work is analytical and validation-focused, its aim is to support a larger long-term vision of extending testing to MCI populations and enabling large-scale community screening programmes.

In the future, the workflow may involve: device distribution by healthcare or community personnel, short-term data collection by users, centralised analysis of data, and identification of at-risk individuals for follow-up testing and treatment. 2. Gait

2.1. Introduction to Gait Concepts

Gait refers to a person’s walking pattern, and it can be analysed through features such as step time, stride time, distance, and stability. Accelerometer data collected during walking produce repeatable signal patterns, where different points correspond to different stages of the gait cycle.

By processing these signals, temporal parameters such as stride time, cadence, and double-support time can be extracted. These parameters have been shown to be sensitive indicators of neurological conditions, including MCI (Mayagoitia et al., 2002; Prisco et al., 2025).

Each gait cycle consists of two main phases: the stance phase, where the foot is in contact with the ground, and the swing phase, where the foot is not in contact with the ground. These phases produce characteristic patterns in accelerometer signals, which can be used as reference signatures for identifying gait events such as heel strike (HS) and toe-off (TO), as shown in Figure 2.

In this project, the primary objective for the gait component is to develop and refine an IMU-based gait analysis pipeline to detect key gait events (ie. heel strike, toe off), extract temporal gait parameters (e.g. stride time and cadence), and validate these results against reference datasets, such as video and Vicon systems.

2.2 Data Collection Methods

Reference Data:

Reference datasets (H019, H020, P048), collected using the sensor developed by the research team, were used as a baseline for validation.

These datasets will be used to confirm the periodicity in the walking signal, while identifying practical limitations of data collection. This includes truncated recordings, sensor lag, and missed initial cycles, that could affect gait event detection in accelerometer signals.

Establishing this baseline on healthy subjects provided confidence that the extracted gait parameters were meaningful before applying the same approach to MCI patients, whose gait patterns are inherently more variable and difficult to interpret.

Self-Collected Data:

The overall approach adopts a two-step validation strategy.

Firstly, a qualitative validation uses video recordings to visually verify that gait events detected in the accelerometer signals correspond to actual walking behaviour.

Thereafter, a quantitative validation is used by comparing events against the industry gold-standard motion-capture system, Vicon, so as to evaluate the numerical accuracy of the detection algorithm.

2.3.1 Qualitative Gait Event Detection and Validation

For self-collected data collection, video recordings (Figure 6) were used as ground-truth reference during gait data collection, allowing the walking sequence to be visually verified against the sensor output. This enabled gait events detected from the signals, such as heel-strike and toe-off, to be labelled correctly.

For the research team's dataset, a synchronised video of patients H019 and H020 (Figure 7) was used to validate gait events. For quantitative validation, gait parameters derived from the accelerometer were compared against the Vicon motion capture system, which serves as the gold-standard benchmark for gait analysis.

2.3.2 Gait Event Detection Approach: Signal Processing

Gait data was collected using inertial measurement units (IMUs) equipped with tri-axial accelerometers, worn at a standardised anatomical location. In this study, the accelerometer IMUs were worn at the ankles, resting above the talus. Prior to feature extraction, an initial inspection of the raw signals was performed to confirm the integrity of each trial before further processing. For example, the duration of collection had to accurately reflect the actual duration in collection, and the consistency and periodicity in the data had to be reflected.

The raw signals exhibited characteristic oscillatory patterns corresponding to periodic gait cycles, with visible peaks and troughs reflecting the heel strike and toe-off events that define each step. However, the presence of noise necessitates a preprocessing stage before performing reliable event detection, consistent with best practices identified in the literature. (Section 2.1.1).

To improve signal quality and ensure reliable feature extraction, filtering techniques were applied. This step is essential as highlighted in prior studies, where preprocessing significantly enhances the accuracy of gait event detection. The filtered signal provides a clearer representation of underlying gait dynamics, forming the basis for subsequent analysis.

To isolate biomechanically meaningful frequencies in the gait signal, a Butterworth filter was applied to the raw accelerometer data. The filter cutoff frequency was selected based on the established literature on human gait, which places the fundamental frequency of walking well below 20 Hz; frequencies above this threshold largely represent noise rather than true movement. This filtering step was critical, as unfiltered signals produced spurious peaks that confounded subsequent event detection.

Following filtering, a peak detection algorithm was applied to identify key gait events, specifically heel strikes and toe offs from the filtered data. Peak prominence and minimum distance between peaks thresholds were tuned to be consistent with the expected cadence range for individuals. This approach is supported by existing literature, which demonstrates that heel strikes are often associated with prominent peaks in acceleration signals due to the impact of the foot with the ground.

Figure 10: Gait Event Identification

Peaks on the filtered accelerometer data graph reflected in Figure 10 correspond to heel strikes and toe strikes with the ground. While the toe-strike peaks are significant in magnitude, they are consistently lower than the heel strike peaks. The heel‑strike event is typically associated with a larger impact force when the foot first contacts the ground, which results in a higher magnitude peak, compared to the subsequent toe‑off event, which involves pushing off the ground rather than absorbing impact (Fellin et al., 2010).

To validate the accuracy of detected gait events, a video comparison analysis was conducted. Recorded walking videos were synchronised with the accelerometer data to allow for visual confirmation that the identified peaks corresponded to actual heel strikes and toe-offs, as described in Section 2.3.1.This step ensures that the implemented algorithm truly reflects gait events and aligns with experimental protocols.

This method identified heel strike and toe-off positions using expected waveform shapes drawn from literature (Figure 15a) (Cleland et al., 2012).

2.5 Data Evaluation

2.5.1 Gait Parameter Analysis

After the detection of gait events, temporal gait parameters were computed for each subject. The key features analysed in this study include step time, stride time, and cadence.

Stride time is defined as the time between successive heel strike peaks of the same foot (i.e. every second peak of the signal). Cadence is defined as the number of steps per minute, and is derived from the mean stride time.

These parameters were extracted for each cycle in a trial, and aggregated across each trial to provide a quantitative of gait parameters for analysis. The standard deviation for each feature was also calculated, as gait variability has been identified as a sensitive marker for cognitive status (Kang et al).

To further validate the extracted parameters, results were cross-referenced against simultaneously collected gyroscope data and against gait parameters computed from a parallel dataset collected in a related component of this research programme. Congruence between these independent calculations provide additional assurance of the reliability and consistency of using accelerometers to provide insights into one’s gait and subsequently diagnosis of MCI.

2.5.2 Comparison of Parameters Between Self-Collected Data and Patient Data

To assess the validity and reliability of the developed method, the extracted gait parameters were compared against values reported in existing literature, including studies such as Kang et al., which provides reference ranges for temporal gait parameters in healthy individuals and individuals with MCI.

The comparison allows for evaluation of whether the observed gait patterns fall within reasonable and expected ranges. Additionally, this comparison assesses whether observed differences between healthy controls and people with MCI are consistent with those mentioned in the literature, as certain gait parameters are known to differ significantly between healthy individuals and those with MCI or Parkinson’s disease.

Across all key parameters, the values derived from healthy participants were in close agreement with the reference data in Kang et al, ensuring credibility to the calculation program.

When comparing healthy controls to the MCI group, differences were observed in stride time, cadence, and double support time. The MCI group exhibited longer stride times and reduced cadence.

These findings suggest that accelerometer-derived temporal gait parameters carry discriminatory potential for distinguishing between healthy and at-risk individuals, supporting the diagnostic feasibility of the proposed system.

2.6 Conclusion

Findings suggest that our IMU-based method was able to capture overall gait patterns and key temporal parameters reasonably well. However, accurate event timing, especially for heel strike, toe-off, and double support, remained the main challenge. The presence of consistent cyclic patterns in both reference and self-collected data supports the validity of the signal-processing approach.

This section demonstrated that accelerometer-based gait analysis is a viable and accessible approach for extracting clinically meaningful temporal parameters, including stride time, step time, cadence, and double support time, from free-walking trials. The signal processing pipeline reliably identified heel strike and toe-off events, supported by video validation and comparison with other derived parameters.

The extracted parameters showed strong agreement with normative values reported in the literature, indicating that this method provides clinically relevant and realistic representations of gait. Although MCI patient data was not available in this study, the results suggest that the approach has potential for future application in clinical populations.

However, accuracy of signal processing may decrease in irregular gait patterns, such as shuffling steps or usage of a walking aid. This may lead to incorrect or ambiguous identification of gait events. Future work should focus on improving event detection in non-ideal walking conditions, for example through adaptive filtering or integration with biomechanical models.

As accelerometers are low-cost, easy to use and sensitive to the rough translational dynamics of an overall gait rhythm, signal-based gait processing is suitable for wide-scale community-level screenings.

3. Speech

3.1 Literature Review

Speech is closely linked to cognitive processes such as memory, attention, and language, making it a sensitive indicator of early cognitive decline. In MCI, subtle changes can be observed, for example, people tend to speak more slowly, pause more often, and show reduced fluency. Their language also becomes simpler, with a more limited vocabulary and less coherent expression. Acoustic features such as pause duration, speech rate, jitter, and phonation stability can reliably distinguish MCI from healthy aging, reflecting the brain’s reduced efficiency in language planning and retrieval. As a result, speech analysis is a promising non-invasive tool for early MCI detection. A summary of the literature findings are reflected in Table 5.

Although extensive literature exists on audio-based detection of MCI, the broader research programme aims to move away from audio-dependent speech assessments, due to concerns about patient privacy and the intrusive nature of open-ended vocal data collection. 3.2 Data Collection Methods and Speech Testing

3.2.1 Sensor Placement

Figure 23: Position of Sensor at Suprasternal Notch

(Jeong et al., 2021)

The research sensor is placed on the neck near the suprasternal notch (SN) directly over the vocal chords. Mechano-acoustic (MA) signals generated during speech attenuate significantly at the skin-air interface, losing energy as vibrations pass from internal tissues into the surrounding air. By mounting the sensor directly on the skin, vibrations are captured before this attenuation occurs, enabling the accelerometer to record distinct vibration signals (Lee et al., 2019).

3.2.2 Choice of Speech Task

Since the accelerometer captures physical vibrations, such as pauses and words, standardisation of the speech task is essential for consistent and comparable data across participants.

Commonly used open-ended tasks, such as picture descriptions, narrative recall, and spontaneous speech, are highly context-dependent, making them difficult to standardise and compare across subjects. Structured tasks offer greater repeatability and signal consistency. Following a review of available protocols (Table 6), the Caterpillar Passage was selected as it gave the most consistent and reliable signals for analysis,

As the study aims to detect MCI through vocal characteristics rather than memory or comprehension performance, a constrained fixed-passage task was the most appropriate choice.

The Caterpillar Passage was developed for the assessment of motor-speech disorders. Its relatively low verbal complexity (Flesh-Kincaid Reading Level of 5.0) ensures the passage can be read by a wide range of participants.

Other key passage attributes include repeated words, enabling analysis of inconsistencies within repeated words; multiple intonation patterns, supporting extraction of prosodic features such as fundamental frequency; and comprehensive inclusion of all English phonemes.

Additional features such as speech rate and pause frequency can also be derived from the collected signal (Patel et al., 2013). These properties make the passage well suited for accelerometer-based mechanoacoustic data collection and analysis in individuals presenting with MCI.

3.3.1 Accelerometer Signal Preprocessing

Figure 28: Accelerometer Data Processing Flow, high level overview

Raw tri-axial accelerometer data from the sensors were collected at the suprasternal notch. The three axes were first combined into a single orientation-independent signal via Euclidean normalisation (Matic et al., 2012), providing a scalar representation of vocal vibration magnitude (Figure 29).

Figure 29: Euclidean Normalisation

A high pass Butterworth filter with a cutoff frequency of 50 Hz was then used to remove low-frequency motion artefact such as swallowing (0-50 Hz), while preserving the vocal frequencies of both male (~100Hz) and female (~200Hz) speakers(Berg et al., 2016).

The Nyquist-Shannon frequency sampling theorem suggests that a sampling frequency has to be twice that of the analog frequency being analysed for an accurate reconstruction of analog to digital signals (Lai, 2003). Hence, a sampling frequency above 400 Hz was used. Using the filtered signal, features such as pauses and fundamental frequency are extractable (Subramanian et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024b).

3.3.2. Feature Extraction Pipeline

Additional signal processing is performed to convert vocal data to a Mel Frequency Plot, a visual representation of speech. This enables further identification of vocal features such as fundamental frequency, pause durations and Jitter (Huang et al., 2024b; Dubey et al., 2020; Kwon et al., 2024) .

Figure 30: Quantitative Parameters (Huang et al., 2024b)

From extensive research, a novel Python-based signal processing pipeline (Figure 31) is developed to extract significant vocal features (pause duration and fundamental frequency) from accelerometer based vocal vibrations.

Figure 31: Vibrational (research) and Audio (ground truth adjacent) Speech Processing Flow using Python

To extract vocal features, the preprocessed accelerometer signal was converted from the time domain to the frequency domain using a Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT). This approach is well suited to non-stationary signals such as speech, as it captures temporal fluctuations in frequency content, essential for analysing connected speech and dynamic vocal features.

Figure 32: (a) Raw Vocal Data (Caterpillar Passage, Research Sensor)

(b) Short Time Fourier Transform Visualisation where (Gao & Yan, 2006)

STFT sliding window parameters were chosen to resolve low vocal frequencies (Figure 33). A minimum window length of 20ms is required to resolve a 50 Hz signal; a 50 ms window with 25 ms (50%) overlap was therefore used for both sensors to ensure at least one full cycle of the lowest vocal frequency could be captured (Rosen et al., 2006).

A larger window size (Figure 33a) favours a large spectral resolution (Figure 33b), due to the ability to analyse a larger number of frequency bins, but results in low temporal resolution  The chosen parameters balance both requirements for speech analysis.

Figure 33: STFT Sliding Window and Resolution Tradeoff
(Ali et al., 2020)

Figure 34: Mel Spectrogram Conversion from STFT
(Habib et al., 2021)

The linear frequency scale output of the STFT is converted into a Mel spectrogram (Figure 34), providing a non linear frequency representation aligned with human auditory perception.

Fundamental frequency (F0) was estimated from the mel spectrogram by identifying the frequency bin with the highest energy within the 50–400 Hz range for each time frame, and averaging across all frames (Figure 35).

Figure 35: Fundamental frequency estimation from vibrational vocal data of “I” speech.

Furthermore, to quantify speech activity, frame level energy was computed by averaging the energy across all frequency bins in the mel spectrogram. As illustrated in Figure 36, high-energy (speech) and low-energy (pause) regions were clearly distinguishable.

To detect speech pauses, frame-level energy was computed by averaging energy across all mel spectrogram frequency bins. High-energy (speech) and low-energy (pause) regions were clearly distinguishable (Figure 36), enabling a threshold-based detection approach.

Empirically determined thresholds of T< 0.002 (research sensor) and T< 0.2 (Metabase sensor) were applied, and continuous low-energy segments exceeding 80ms were classified as significant pauses.

Detected pause segments, highlighted in red in Figure 36, were subsequently categorised as short (80–180 ms) or long (> 180 ms) for further analysis.

 

Figure 36: Pause detection Analysis.

3.3.3. Discussion

Current research indicates that individuals with MCI demonstrate a significantly higher frequency of both long and short pauses compared to cognitively healthy participants, and that elevated pause counts correlate with amyloid-β accumulation and tau burden (He et al., 2025). Therefore, pauses in speech are the primary focus of the current analysis pipeline. Using the above mentioned vocal processing pipeline on healthy participant, pause occurrences and durations were successfully extracted.

Figure 37: Pauses Detected by Praat Software.
Where green show significant pauses, red shows insignificant pauses

During analysis for ground truth features, initial Praat pause detection using the time domain alone produced a number of insignificant pauses (Figure 37, red circles). This was resolved by incorporating frequency-domain screening, treating any sustained low-frequency activity at approximately 50 Hz as a pause. Following this refinement and manual cross-checking of the output, the total pause counts across all three pipelines were brought into close agreement.

Time frames bounded by white lines are the points where pauses were detected. These time pause durations were extracted and plotted to visualise the distribution (Figure 38).

Figure 38: Pause Detections – Vibration Research Pipeline, Audio Pipeline, Ground Truth

Detected pause durations were extracted and plotted to visualise their distribution (Figure 39). All three pipelines produced a bimodal distribution, consistent with research publications that describe pause behaviour in speech follows a bimodal log-normal pattern, separating pause intervals into short (80-180 ms) and long (>180 ms) pauses (He et al., 2025; Hale et al., 2025).

Figure 39:  Pause duration bimodal distribution of (a) Praat (b) Audio Python code (c) Research Python code

However, upon analysis of the bimodal distribution, the vibration-based pipeline showed slightly reduced sensitivity to short pauses relative to the audio-based methods.

Further analysis using Praat software revealed that short pauses (80–180 ms) typically correspond to inter-word gaps in the Caterpillar Passage, many of which involve voiceless consonants. Unlike voiced consonants, which produce detectable vocal cord vibrations, voiceless consonants involve minimal vocal fold engagement and allow air to flow freely, generating little to no vibrational signal at the suprasternal notch (Zhang, 2016).

Voiced consonants use the vocal chords by tightening and relaxing the muscles as you speak, resulting in vibrations. However, voiceless letters/consonants do not use the vocal chords to produce the sounds, instead they are low tension and allow air to flow freely through.

Figure 44: Caterpillar Passage, Voiced and Non-Voiced Consonants

For example, at 49.6–50.3 s, the spoken words "it went" include the voiceless /t/, which was misclassified as part of a pause by the vibration sensor. Similarly, at 20.4s, the /wh/ preceding a word boundary was not captured, leading to an overestimated pause duration. This explains the observed discrepancy in short pause detection between the vibration and audio pipelines. However, a larger sample population (n >= 30) is required to conclude the preliminary analysis.

As both short and long pause frequencies increase with MCI (He et al., 2025; Hale et al., 2025), and given that long pauses are detected reliably by the vibration sensor, long pause duration and frequency are used as the primary features for subsequent MCI-related analysis. A larger sample (n ≥ 30) will be required to draw broader conclusions from these preliminary findings.

3.4.2 Limitations and Improvements

The speech analysis component demonstrates the feasibility of using mechano-acoustic vibration signals to extract meaningful speech-related features in a non-invasive and privacy-preserving manner. Features such as pause duration, fundamental frequency and signal amplitude patterns were successfully derived from the recorded signals and were found to be consistent with expected characteristics of natural speech production.

However, there are several limitations at the current stage of development. Firstly, the dataset is limited to a small number of healthy participants, which restricts the generalisability of the findings. Additionally, there has not been data collected from individuals with MCI. Therefore the ability of the extracted features to distinguish between healthy and cognitively impaired populations cannot yet be evaluated.

Furthermore, unlike gait analysis, the speech component lacks a direct quantitative ground-truth reference, and validation is therefore limited to qualitative assessment and comparison with established literature. The accuracy of the extracted features may also be affected by variations in sensor placement and external noise, which introduces additional uncertainty into the measurements.

Future work will focus on expanding the dataset to include a larger cohort of healthy participants, as well as individuals diagnosed with MCI. This will allow for the establishment of baseline feature distributions and enable meaningful comparisons between population groups. There could also be an integration of machine learning approaches, whereby speech signals can be used to train classification models capable of distinguishing between healthy and MCI individuals. This is a critical step towards translating feature extraction into a practical screening tool.

Further improvements will also involve enhancing the robustness of the system under real-world conditions, including variations in sensor placement and environmental noise. In the longer term, the integration of speech features with gait-based parameters may be explored to develop a multimodal screening framework, potentially improving sensitivity to early cognitive decline.

Overall, while the current work establishes a foundational approach for vibration-based speech analysis, further validation and development are required to support its application as a scalable, non-invasive screening tool for MCI.

4. Summary

Figure 45: Multimodal Feature Database (Gait & Speech)

This project explored whether accelerometer-based gait and speech signals can be used as part of a non-invasive and accessible screening approach for MCI.

For gait, accelerometer signals were used to detect heel strike and toe off events and extract temporal parameters such as stride time , cadence and double support. Comparison with video and Vicon showed that the refined method could capture the general gait pattern , although precise event timing remained challenging. For speech, vocal vibrations were successfully processed to extract features such as pause durations and fundamental frequency that matched the ground truth.

Overall, the findings support the feasibility of using accelerometer-based signals to derive meaningful features of gait and speech from healthy individuals, while showing that further validation on larger datasets and MCI participants is still needed before practical screening use.
